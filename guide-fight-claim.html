How to Fight a Workers' Comp Claim

Not every claim is legitimate, and employers have the right to contest the ones that aren't. Here is how to build a case that holds.

Most workers' comp claims are real and should be paid promptly. But some are exaggerated, some are not work-related, and a few are outright fabricated. Because a claim is decided on the evidence, the employer who documents and investigates well can contest the questionable ones, and the employer who does nothing pays for all of them. This is a practical guide to doing it right.

Know the red flags

Contesting a claim starts with recognizing which claims deserve a closer look. Common indicators include:

  • Delayed or late reporting, especially a claim reported hours or days after the shift.
  • No witnesses to an incident that should have had them.
  • Injuries first reported on a Monday morning, or right after discipline or a layoff notice.
  • An account of the injury that changes over time.
  • Refusal of treatment at first, followed by an attorney and escalating care.
  • Behavior inconsistent with the claimed injury, on social media or in person.

One flag is not proof. A cluster of them is a reason to investigate before you pay.

Move fast, because evidence is perishable

The window to gather the facts closes quickly. Memories fade, stories get polished, and records get harder to obtain. The employers who win contested claims are the ones who act in the first hours and days, not weeks later.

1. Report and document immediately

Capture a complete first report the moment an injury is claimed: who, what, when, where, the exact mechanism, and who was present. A structured intake removes the gaps a questionable claim depends on.

2. Get the initial medical records

The first medical account, taken at urgent care or the ER before a claim exists, is often the truest version of what happened. It is where a "workplace" injury sometimes turns out to have a very different origin. Getting those initial records is frequently the single most decisive step.

3. Lock down witness statements

Signed statements taken while memories are fresh are powerful and hard to walk back. Talk to everyone who was near the scene, including those who saw the employee before and after, not just at the alleged moment.

4. Build the timeline

Lay the facts out in order: shift times, the alleged incident, the report, the treatment, and the behavior around each. Inconsistencies, like an injury time that predates the shift, surface on their own once the timeline is clear.

5. Work the carrier and defense together

Give your carrier and defense counsel a complete, organized file, and hold them accountable to act on it. A well-documented claim gives the defense the ammunition to deny, request an independent medical exam, or move to dismiss.

What a defensible claim looks like

The goal is not to fight every claim. It is to make every claim defensible, so that when one deserves to be contested, the evidence is already there. Complete documentation, signed statements, the initial records, and a clean timeline turn a hunch into a case.
