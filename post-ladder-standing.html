The Ladder Was Still Standing

A back injury, a cane at the doctor's office, and a surveillance camera that told the real story.

Claim Files is a series of anonymized case studies from CompShield's desk. Names, employers, locations, and identifying details have been changed. The facts of how the claim was investigated are real.

He arrived at the independent medical exam leaning on a cane, moving like a man in serious pain. He told the examiner a ladder had fallen on him.

There was one problem with that story. When the accident scene was checked, the ladder was still standing upright.

The claim

An electrician for a New York contractor said he was hurt on the job, pulling electrical conduit while working from a stepladder. A coworker found him on the floor. He was taken to the hospital by ambulance. The carrier accepted the claim for a low-back strain and began paying temporary total disability, the wage-replacement benefit for injured workers who cannot work. He hired an attorney and requested a hearing.

On paper, another routine lost-time claim. Underneath, the file had holes from the start.

The cracks

None of these prove anything on their own. Together, they were more than enough reason to look closer.

  • No one saw it happen. The incident was unwitnessed, and there was no video of the fall.
  • He was where he wasn't supposed to be. Per the site foreman, the claimant was working in an area he was not authorized to be in.
  • He had been on the job for 41 days.
  • He refused to give a statement and would not communicate with the company.
  • When help arrived, he declined the private ambulance and specifically asked for the city fire department instead.

The mechanism that didn't match

At the exam, the claimant told the physician a ladder had fallen on him. The scene said otherwise. The ladder was found upright and undisturbed, and a ladder that falls on someone does not set itself back on its feet.

The account he gave the doctor did not match the physical evidence, and it did not match how a coworker found him. That is exactly the kind of gap a biomechanical review can take apart: does the injury on the imaging actually fit the event the claimant describes?

The camera

Then came the surveillance.

On the day of his exam, the claimant arrived using a cane. After he left the office, investigators kept watching. Within the same day he was recorded driving, refueling his own vehicle without the cane, walking around the city, pushing a stroller, and shopping, showing none of the difficulty he had displayed inside the exam room. An earlier round of surveillance had not found him at the physical-therapy appointments his claim depended on.

The cane, it turned out, was for the doctor.

The turn

The picture that emerged was not of a disabled worker. It was of a claim that performed disability on cue and set it down the moment the exam ended. The wage-replacement benefits were suspended, and the claim moved into active dispute, with the carrier's investigators, defense counsel, and CompShield lining the evidence up side by side: the scene, the mechanism, and the video.

The lesson for employers

Surveillance has a bad reputation, but it exists for a reason. A claim is decided on evidence, and some claims cannot survive being watched. The strongest defense here was not one silver bullet. It was the stack: the unwitnessed incident, the unauthorized area, the refusal to give a statement, the mechanism that contradicts the scene, and the footage that contradicts the exam. Any one is a question. Together they are an answer.

At CompShield, that is the standard on every questionable claim we touch:

  • Get the scene and the mechanism right, early. Before the story is polished.
  • Put the claimant's account next to the physical evidence. Inconsistencies surface on their own.
  • Use the IME, and a biomechanical expert when needed, to test the injury against the alleged event.
  • Let surveillance confirm or refute what the file already suggests.

Exaggerated and fraudulent claims are not just an expense. They raise your reserves, inflate your experience mod, and drive your premiums up for years. The ones worth fighting are worth fighting with evidence.
