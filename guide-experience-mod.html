How to Lower Your Workers' Comp Experience Mod

Your experience mod is a multiplier on your premium. Here is how it works, and seven proven ways to bring it down.

Few numbers affect an employer's insurance cost as much as the experience modification rate, and few are as poorly understood. Your mod is a single factor that multiplies your workers' comp premium up or down based on your past losses. Lower it, and you pay less for the same coverage, every year, for years.

Here is how it works and what actually moves it.

What the experience mod is

The mod compares your company's actual losses to the losses expected for a business of your size and class codes. The result is a factor centered on 1.00, which is the industry-average baseline.

  • A mod above 1.00 is a debit. A 1.25 mod means you pay 25% more than an average peer.
  • A mod below 1.00 is a credit. A 0.85 mod means you pay 15% less.

Two details surprise most employers. First, the formula weights claim frequency more heavily than severity, so several small claims can hurt you more than one large one. Second, losses stay in your rating window for about three years, so today's claims are tomorrow's premium.

Seven ways to lower it

1. Report every claim fast

Claims reported the same day cost less and are far easier to defend. Delay invites disputes, missing documentation, and claim growth, all of which feed your mod. Same-day, structured intake is the single cheapest lever you have.

2. Manage and contest claims aggressively

Every dollar of loss that shouldn't be paid still counts against your mod. Investigating questionable claims, disputing what doesn't belong, and closing files faster keeps losses off your record. The claims you never should have paid are pure mod damage.

3. Return people to work

Indemnity, the wage-replacement portion of a claim, is the most expensive part and a major mod driver. A light-duty or modified-duty program gets employees back sooner, cuts indemnity, and shrinks the loss that hits your rating.

4. Reduce claim frequency

Because the formula punishes frequency, cutting the number of small claims pays off out of proportion to their size. Targeted safety and loss-prevention work on your most common injuries is mod strategy, not just risk management.

5. Watch your reserves

Open claims are counted at their reserved value, not just what has been paid. Reserves set too high, and never revisited, inflate your losses on paper. Reviewing and challenging reserves keeps your mod honest.

6. Audit the mod worksheet for errors

Mod worksheets contain mistakes more often than employers realize: wrong class codes, misstated payroll, duplicate claims, or claims that were later denied but never removed. Each error can cost you real premium. A worksheet audit before renewal is one of the highest-return hours you can spend.

7. Get ahead of renewal

Because losses roll off after about three years, timing matters. Knowing which claims are about to drop out, and projecting your mod before renewal, lets you plan and negotiate from a position of knowledge instead of surprise.

The bottom line

Your experience mod is not fixed. It is the scoreboard of how well your claims are managed, and it responds to discipline. Faster reporting, harder claim defense, and a real return-to-work program will bend it down, and the savings compound year after year.
